About

Smart Triaging is a design project that revolves around designing interactions for autonomous technologies. The team focused on how autonomous technologies could be used to streamline emergency ambulance services so to meet the ever-growing demand. This resulted in the conception of Smart Triaging: a futuristic triaging smartwatch application that automatically scans the user's body and alerts them of any medical injuries, allowing them to call for an ambulance for that injury straight from their smartwatch.

Call an ambulance for triaged injuries and track its live status

The application works in conjunction with a proposed autonomous ecosystem. When the user requests for an ambulance via their smartwatch, an alarm will be sent to the ambulance service. The system autonomously analyses the received data and decides whether the ambulance needs to be equipped with appropriate specialised equipment. Data regarding the user's injury, medical history, will be available for the EMTs to access on route within the ambulance.

The concept was designed to improve communication between all involved parties, maintain and improve service quality, as well as help ambulance services better allocate resources to meet the increasing demand for these services.

System Overview

Allowing personalised use and ease of access, a smartwatch interface was designed for triaging injuries and making efficient emergency calls. Paired with a smartphone, users sign up and manage their account via a mobile application, information that is also accessible via the smartwatch application.

Design Process

Research

A mixture of primary and secondary research was conducted via interviews, surveys and literature reviews. By analysing the researched data two key problem areas arose, serving the basis for our idea conception stage:

  • the importance of communication with all related parties, and
  • the strain non urgent calls were placing on limited resources.

    • “proper communication amongst all involved personnel is vital to improve joint decision making, and helps enable a shared sense of situational awareness amongst co-workers (Harmsen et al., 2016).”

    Research Survey Data

    Ideation

    Taking these key themes into consideration, the ideation stage begun through various brainstorming, iterative design sessions and thorough discussions. Implementing autonomous technologies was a challenging task that resulted in a continual shift in focus between different user groups. Should the solution be designed for the paramedics, or for the regular callers? How prominent of a role should autonomous technologies be playing in the forefront of the solution? These were complex questions that we attempted to solve by drafting up numerous ideas, and meticulously discussing the pros and cons of each.

    Brainstorming Sessions
    Solution Variations

    In addition to two the key themes, factors of necessity, possibility, and feasibility were carefully evaluated with each idea. With such a complicated and unfamiliar topic as autonomous technologies, often the various ideas found themselves lacking, and dangerously treading the lines of ambitious innovation and science fiction, something that needed to be avoided. The ideation stage was mentally taxing on the team as we struggled to come up with a solution that satisfied all these areas, as well as something that we as a team could personally be proud of.

    Through a harsh continual process of iteration and feedback from tutors, ideas were cut, added, and refined. This resulted in multiple features that we believed would complement each other well in an overarching system. By switching our focus to indirectly providing assistance to solve the problem areas rather than trying to automate the actual paramedic service, we were led to the design of our autonomous ecosystem, triggered by a digital application.

    Concept Storyboard Design

    Testing

    Multiple rounds of testing were conducted with a range of participants, some professionally involved in the medical industry. Initial tests were conducted on paper prototypes of two competing interfaces, a mobile and smartwatch interface. By assigning the participants various tasks of navigating both interfaces, feedback was collected via their comments, errors, general observations of their behaviour, as well as an additional survey for each design iteration. The mobile interface initially also possessed the capability to triage injuries, but was later revoked due to the smartwatch receiving a more positive reception for its convenience.

    Initial feedback revealed underlying issues with screen flow, choice of icons and other design elements, as well as the need for further documentation. Analysing this feedback allowed us to iterate further upon the designs.

    Interface Design Iteration Two

    By creating a high fidelity mock-up, rounds of testing were conducted where the feedback began to see a change of focus on more detailed elements such as wording and aesthetic design choices. Applying the additional feedback, we were able to reach our final design.

    There is much room for the interfaces to be improved upon. While many rounds of testing were conducted with our interfaces, testing and user feedback never really ends. Feedback received from our final round of testing suggested potential for further expansion in functionality and account personalisation, as well as providing more detailed medical information.

    Conclusions

    This was a very challenging project that the team initially approached with much apprehension, but concluded with feelings of content. In addition to the unexplored world of autonomous services, ambulance services was not an area we possessed extensive knowledge in, thus widening the scope of unknown territory. A few regretful mistakes were made, such as interview and testing rounds that could've been more smoothly executed, but all in all we believe that this was a successful project. Only through the 12 weeks of continual civil discourse and fluctuations of success, would we be exposed to these valuable learning experiences.
