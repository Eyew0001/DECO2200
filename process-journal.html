PROCESS JOURNAL

Photo by delfi de la Rua on Unsplash

Introduction

As the population continues to grow, the demand for ambulance services is predicted to increase in a similar fashion. To keep up with this ever-growing demand, the current ambulance service system must be revised and further assessed. As we slowly start to welcome an autonomous technology driven future, there is potential to improve ambulance services even further by streamlining the procedure and improve response and further benefiting society. This project focuses on exploring how autonomous technologies can be integrated in emergency ambulance services to improve the overall process and experience.

Background Research

To better understand the context of our study and identify all problem areas, a combination of literary, interview and survey research regarding the current state of ambulance services was conducted. Mapping out all stakeholders involved with ambulance services brought us three major user groups: medical staff involved with ambulance services, caretakers, and regular users who have experienced calling or using ambulance services before. To cover as much ground as possible, each member individually chose one user group for the first stage of research, where I covered caretakers.

Research: First Stage

Affinity diagram identifying two key themes

6 semi-structured interviews were conducted with 5 aged care workers over the phone and 1 primary school teacher in person to gain qualitative data on the perspective of those who are legally responsible for the wellbeing of another person. Questions regarding their routine, experience and personal opinions on any services they've received and what factors contribute to a successful service were asked, providing further insight into any latent or tacit needs.

As I review the responses, part of me questions how successful this first stage of research really was. Majority of the interviewees answered very similarly, which could just mean there is a sense of consistency with aged care practices. However there was little to no differences in personal experiences, as if they all worked for the same place. If I were to pinpoint the main reason for this, it would definitely be my flawed interviewing skills. Nervousness when interviewing strangers, lack of preparation as well as experience were probably the main factors that contributed to a lacklustre interview. I believe that better choice of more open ended questions and active listening skills would have brought out deeper responses.

Additionally due to their busy schedules, I had to resort to conducting the aged care interviews over the phone, which in my opinion affected both the atmosphere of the interview and my analysis of the feedback. As I could not determine facial observations or read body language, it was hard for me to create a conversation that flowed naturally, instead feeling very one sided. I was also very conscious of time because of how busy they were, which led me to rushing the interview. However I have to make do with what I have.

Findings

Analysis of the generally positive feedback resulted in the emergence of a central theme: the importance of communication and resources. Interviewees responded that "they more or less communicate" and "always give a time frame". They also knew how to cooperate with the paramedics, knowing what vitals and signs to look for helped speed up the treatment process and improve efficiency. The interviewees were pleased overall with their experiences with ambulance services thus far, and efficient communication and skills played a big part in this positive experience.

Compiling this data with the rest of the team led to similar findings throughout all three user groups of the importance of communication and resources. Ambulance workers believed that efficiency was dependent on the stability of communication methods between all parties involved, and that ambulances being equipped with the bare minimum equipment to cover all situations potentially affected the quality of their services. Similarly, users who had experienced calling or using an ambulance before found that minimal communication between themselves and the workers lead to insecurity and anxiety as they were not properly informed of the situation, and found that some preferred not to use the service in less urgent situations due to the cost.

Research: Second Stage

To support the primary data, I conducted further secondary research regarding these two emergent themes by writing a literature review. Past studies presented statistical data and key results from not only caretakers but a variety of perspectives, which provided deeper insight into the two main areas of communication and resources. Within these areas, specific issues arose:

  • majority of emergency calls being deemed non-urgent or low acuity,
  • and the vital role communication plays in improving preparation, teamwork and service.

Findings

Findings found that majority of cases were classified as medically unnecessary (Camasso-Richardson, et al., 1997) but are more likely to believe that their illness required immediate care within an hour (Pearson et al., 2018), which ultimately affects the availability of services for high acuity cases. Strain on the emergency response system can lead to communication problems manifesting in paramedic teamwork (O’Meara, 2003) in an environment where proper communication amongst all involved personnel is vital to improve joint decision making and collaboration (Harmsen et al., 2016), and helps keep not just the patient feeling secure but also the workers (Norden et al., 2014).

Research: Third Stage

A survey was conducted to gain a deeper understanding into the work experience of paramedics. The quantitative data collected would help provide additional support to the secondary research as well as provide statistical data specific to the key problem areas. Responses were collected by posting to subreddit communities and survey exchange websites, since I had a detrimental lack of personal connections with paramedics and ambulance workers. This resulted in a successful collection of 38 responses.

Findings

Results reiterated the importance of communication, with 71% stating knowing the chief complaint as the most important piece of information to know when dispatched, and 63% also stating location as the most important. Order of importance varied across responses however, with one even stating "Correct location is paramount, chief complaint is a luxury."

The issue of low acuity calls was brought up again, with results showing that 48% of the 38 respondents found their services often not actually being medically required, with 49% feeling frustrated because of this.

Comment by participant justanaveragepenguin

A few comments were made regarding the survey and the questions I asked, and I realise that multiple interpretations can affect the consistency of my data. In regards to the question "How often in your career have you got to the scene and your services were not actually required", one responder commented "I answered with 'cancelled on arrival' in mind. The number would have been much higher if I answered and considered services not required to mean - the patient only wanted a bandaid/panadol/to tell their teenage of for not getting home by curfew." While I in fact made the question with the latter interpretation in mind, the former interpretation helped me identify further reasons for ambulance services not being needed.

As I was looking for data related to my two key areas of non-urgent cases and communication, my questions typically stayed within those themes. However because of this, one responder commented that it seemed my questions created the assumption that all paramedic work was related to emergency trauma responses when in reality most patients aren't sick and won't need any prehospital management. This data was in fact very valuable as it did support the key findings of my literature review, but it did mean that I did not properly explain the intention of my survey, so (ironically) better communication was needed in relaying the survey's purpose to help participants understand and respond more accurately.

Identifying Problem Areas

After conducting our individual research, we compiled all of the research data and understood that the key issues we found individually were common with everyone on the team. The first being that, majority of calls are classified as non-urgent, which ultimately puts unnecessary strain on emergency service resources. Additionally communication was found to be a vital contributor to a positive experience. Whether it be communication between dispatchers and patients, or between paramedics and dispatchers, lack of communication often resulted in confusion and delays in service.

Using these key themes we conducted a brainstorming session regarding characteristics that we believe important to consider in our solution, and how it would tie in with user needs. Our combined research contains a wide range of perspectives so mapping out our thoughts on the same page helped provide a visualisation of how various themes and characteristics link to one another.

Ideation

Ideation brainstorming session one

After figuring out the user needs, the ideation stage begun for potential solutions. While churning out as many ideas as possible, each member added their solutions to the page. No matter how improbable they may seem, these ideas would serve as a warm up exercise to allow us to get in the right mood and context, and uncover as many possible solutions as possible.

Each member chose their two best solutions and wrote a brief summary describing their chosen concepts. Passing the page along, members then added features they thought would improve the idea, continually building and expanding upon the base of the original design. Pros and cons were also added and discussed, to give us an idea of what the benefits of choosing this solution would be, as well as the possible limitations we would face. After thorough discussion of these pros and cons and through process of elimination, our 3 optimal solutions were chosen. The chosen ideas were:

  • an ambulance triaging mobile application,
  • a smart ambulance, and
  • an autonomous equipment allocation system.

We believe that these ideas covered both key points the range of these ideas covered both key points. A mobile application was suitable for covering interactions for users wishing to use ambulance services and help determine what emergencies were considered urgent. A smart autonomous ambulance was proposed with the concept of helping to efficiently distribute resources of paramedics and drivers to focus on more urgent calls when needed, and an autonomous equipment allocation system would assist ambulance workers in improving resources and their services.

However after further discussion and feedback from our tutor, we felt that these ideas were still lacking as despite basing our ideas around our initial mind map of user needs and issues, we found the incorporation of our central theme, autonomous technologies, was very minimal for all of the designs.

This realisation really dampened the team's morale. After spending a good 6 hours previously coming up with our 3 ideas, the fact that these ideas were not suitable led us to worry about the future of this project. Additionally, coming up with ideas was mentally exhausting as there's a limit to our innovation capabilities, but nonetheless we had no choice. Thus, another round of ideation brainstorming took place, again each choosing their best idea to create a new set of 3. By analysing each of our ideas, we were fortunately able to decide on our new 3:

  • medical chips,
  • AI recognition street cameras, and
  • autonomous traffic lights.
Ideation brainstorming session two

Medical chips were chosen for the potential of creating a concept that was personalised to the users, street cameras for implementing a public presence that was accessible to everyone, and autonomous traffic lights were chosen as Annie (fellow team member) saw potential in implementing this feature as she came across it in her individual research.

After thoroughly reviewing these new ideas, we saw potential to integrate these features with our original 3 ideas.

Reflections

These rounds of ideation and brainstorming consumed more time than we had hoped, becoming definite setbacks for the team, towards both the schedule and our motivation. A considerable amount of time was spent coming up with a sound solution that considered not only the problem areas that we discovered, but also implemented autonomous technology. While the current state of autonomous technology is seeing slow but steady advancements, the idea has yet to become properly implemented in society.

One thing we had to learn was to accept the futuristic nature of the context and to allow ourselves to think broader. Despite encouraging as many solutions as possible no matter how farfetched they may seem, when selecting probable features it was difficult to determine the line that separated the worlds of innovation, and downright science fiction. Due to our unfamiliarity with autonomous technology and its possibilities, we were unsure of what seemed plausible in the coming future. Additionally, novelty and originality was a personal goal for the team, even if there was no specific requirement for such. This led to hours of civil discourse within the group as we thoroughly discussed each and every idea's potential and limitations.

In spite of all the time it took to finally come to a convincing agreement, all of these discussions really benefited us in the end, as it forced us to think deeper about each idea and contemplate various factors such as the necessity, the possibility, and the feasibility of these ideas. Our analysis made sure that we put extra thought into every aspect, so that we could only pick our best options.

Final Solution

Autonomous system storyboard design

By incorporating various features, we decided upon creating an autonomous ecosystem.

Via interacting with an interface, patients' injuries are triaged, prompting them to call ambulances when appropriate. The patient’s location, injury data and medical history would be analysed by the system to make autonomous decisions. Based on these decisions, any special equipment needed would be autonomously allocated to an autonomous ambulance via a rail system. This provides paramedics with vital information on the patient, and ensures the paramedics are thoroughly prepared before arrival.

On route, the system will analyse and generate the most efficient route to reach the patient, changing traffic lights and improving traffic flow in the ambulance’s favour. The system additionally identifies the nearest hospital that would be able to take in the patient, creating a route from the scene to the hospital.

Interfaces

It was decided that to benefit staff and help improve quality of service, we needed to target regular callers as they are the main reason that these services are needed in the first place, and are the main users that dictate the flow of the system. Three interfaces were designed to interact with the system:

  • Futuristic street lamps that used sensors and cameras to autonomously detect and triage certain injuries in public areas, with this solution taking much influence from AI recognition.
  • A smartwatch application that would use sensors to detect bodily injuries on the wearer, with this solution taking much influence from the idea of medical chips.
  • Mobile triaging application that would use AI recognition to scan and detect any injuries or illnesses.

Complications

Upon further development and discussion, we discovered that our streetlamp design would not be able to cater for indoor cases. Though initially designed for outdoor use, a large number of calls do take place indoor, such as 9.7% of calls being made up of elderly fall patients. For the reason that this idea could not cover indoor areas, it was decided that we could no longer continue with this design.

User Testing

For both solutions various screens were designed and tested with various users. Pre-testing surveys were conducted to identify our participants and their familiarity with the context presented, such as their experience with smartwatches and health related apps. Post-testing surveys were also held to collect statistical ratings of our designs.

Think aloud testing was conducted to capture the organic thought process each participant had when interacting with the designs. Each participant was asked to complete a set of tasks where their time, errors, comments and observations were recorded as feedback. Three stages of testing were held with 20 unique users overall, with some participating in numerous rounds. This allowed us to collect feedback from new perspectives as well evaluations on any adjustments we made. It was also ensured that a participant from the medical industry was involved with testing for each round. These comparisons from a wide range of participants allowed for deeper insights into how users interacted with our interfaces.

Users tested with paper prototypes made to emulate our designed interfaces. While we attempted to visually represent the screens as accurately as was possible, there was still some difficulty in effectively representing digital interactions on paper for aspects such as camera scanning functions and animation elements. However despite this we believe were still able to collect thorough and rich data in spite of this issue.

User Testing: First Stage

For the first round of testing we tested with a total of 9 unique users.

The first round helped us identify many flaws with our mobile application. Issues with screen flow were presented when users were asked to triage for other people. Many found it difficult to comprehend the need for logging out, and failed to recognise logged in statuses. Only after explanations were given did they understand the need for this.

During testing I made a personal decision only to explain the bare minimum of the application to my 3 participants in order to get a natural reaction and for them to compare their expectations of the app to their own frame of reference, as well as simulate potential situations. However this ended up creating problems when I realised how flawed our design was. In hindsight, our intended process to log out when triaging others was unnecessarily confusing. While it's important that user's personal details don't get mixed up, at the time of conception logging out seemed like the best option. However it's quite obvious that this was not going to work out well without any sort of explanation. So this definitely needs to be heavily revised.

Due to the limited screen size of the smartwatch the main task we faced was designing an interface that was easy to navigate and access. While these issues were considered, navigation problems still presented themselves, with majority of users not being experienced with smartwatches and so found difficulty navigating due to the lack of instructions provided. However as smartwatches are catered for the wearer where triaging for different people was not an option, participants were quicker in understanding and using the smartwatch application, and were actually more in favour of the smartwatch. This discovery was quite interesting, and brought up the question of if whether or not revising the mobile application screen flow would change these results.

User Testing: Second Stage

For the second round the solutions were iterated upon with the screen flow being adjusted and icons being redesigned, in which we received more balanced results this time. 11 tests were conducted overall, with some participants being brought back from the first round of testing. I also learnt my mistakes of not explaining enough, so for my new participants I touched on a few more important features but still tried to not give away too much information.

The mobile application saw a redesign in the triage process, which found much greater success than the first iteration. As we removed the need to log out and created a home page, users found it easier to understand switch between triaging for themselves and others. With the addition of a few new screens and change in screen flow, users found themselves lacking sufficient explanations of certain features. Users felt that more information was required, which tied into the need for further help and documentation.

Other than a few navigation issues, we still found that users found task completion easier on smartwatches and were still more in favour of the smartwatch, mainly because the idea of wearables being used for medical purposes was believable as is common in society today. Thus users found themselves understanding the convenience of the smartwatch concept more than the mobile application. And for that reason we ultimately decided for our final solution to be the smartwatch application.

However smartwatch applications must have a mobile counterpart and do not typically work as a standalone smartwatch application. So we decided to retain the login and account creation process of the mobile application. Our final solution would now consist of using the mobile application to create an account, while using the smartwatch for triaging and calling functions.

User Testing: Third Stage

The screen designs went through a final round of iteration to improve clarity and create a high fidelity wireframe design that implemented specific aesthetic choices such as fonts and colour. This was done by using universal icons, as it was pointed out that some instructions such as 'OK' and 'Next' seemed vague in interpretation of what screen or direction it would take them to. Additionally, initial onboarding tutorial screens explaining the app functions was added to the mobile application. This round we saw ourselves conducting 10 tests.

Users were successful with navigating their way with both the mobile and smartwatch counterparts. Jiyoung's (fellow team member) participants working in the medical industry advised for wording and visual hierarchy to be revised, as the choice of wording presented seemed to be misleading or open to misinterpretation, as well as believing that certain text elements are of higher priority than the icons.

While we did have some new participants who had not seen these designs before, it is a wonder why a few of the feedback elements received were not brought up earlier. It is not really known why problems with wording did not come up in earlier iterations, though it could be because during the first few rounds participants were focusing solely on the interactions rather than the text, or just simply that these were new participants. Additionally, each iteration presents problems with the first aid icon, as users continually struggle to interpret the icons we've chosen. This is somewhat frustrating as we believe the final icon chosen is as straightforward as it gets and would prefer not to spell it out for them using text, but it seems this we have no choice but to resort to this.

User Testing: Survey

Participants were asked to rank overall ease of navigation and effectiveness on a 5 point Likert scale and these results (in addition to actual comments) were used to determine the overall reception to each of the interfaces and which of the two was more favoured. While we believe we carefully chose the right areas to inspect, it is possible that a few of the survey questions were a bit difficult to assess. Likert scale questions such as "how effective do you think this would be in a non-lethal emergency?" or "how easy was it to navigate?" were honestly at times difficult to gauge for a few users. Though these responses were necessary for our evaluations, it is possible that users could not accurately answer due to themes such as "effectiveness" being hard to represent through a 5 point Likert scale. The data may have been more accurate had we worded the questions better.

Interactive Prototype

Overall the whole process seemed quite successful despite our initial setbacks. Plentiful data was collected throughout our testing, and we believe that participants had a positive response to the designs so far. Based off of the feedback received from our final testing stage, appropriate modifications were made to our high fidelity wireframes to develop the high-fidelity mock-up using InVision and emulate the proper digital interactive experience as accurately as possible.

User Testing

We once again conducted casual tests for our interactive prototype with various users and iterated over 3 rounds of testing. These tests were conducted by getting the participants to interact with the digital InVision prototype. This time we also managed to receive further feedback from a few tutors from our university, who due to their experience in their crafts, helped us identify various design flaws and potentials.

For our mobile application it was suggested for more help and documentation to be added to various pages, for further adjustments to once again be made to our choice of wording and phrasings, as well as actually providing functionality to a few of our buttons so to flesh out the prototype more. Furthermore the addition of an emergency contact feature was recommended for our smartwatch application.

Reflections

After taking all of the feedback we received over the three rounds into consideration, we ended up with our final prototype. However there is still room for improvements and expansion upon functions. We discussed the potential for storing and displaying users' regular GP for easy contact, however due to time constraints and InVision limitations we believed that showing nearby GPs was sufficient for now.

Additionally, the inclusion of a colour blind mode was added due to feedback from one of our medical professional participants (Stephanie) to improve accessibility. Personally I found this inclusion quite questionable, as I don't believe our design to be so heavily dependent on colour that would warrant the need for a colour blind mode, and believe that other disabilities could've been demonstrated instead. However a medical professional clearly possesses more knowledge in this subject than I or anyone else in this team, so the function was included, and demonstrated that the list of accessibility features could potentially be expanded upon in the future.
