PROTOTYPE

Photo by delfi de la Rua on Unsplash

Introduction

The demand for NSW ambulance services has seen a minimum 2% increase each year for the past five years and is predicted to grow (Audit Office, 2017). ‘Smart Triaging’ is an application that has been developed for the smartwatch and smartphone to combat this growing issue.

Research

Two major themes were found across our research:

  • The importance of communication between the calling party, paramedics and dispatchers.
  • Non-urgent calls are placing a strain on already limited resources.

‘Smart Triaging’ targets less urgent emergency situations, to allow better allocation of resources with a focus from the user’s end.

This is achieved through the concept of ‘self triaging’, an idea that involves the user being able to gain information on their own by using autonomous technology. Smartwatches are wearable devices that have become quickly integrated into society with heavy development in health associated projects. Due to these reasons, the smartwatch was chosen as the main device as user testing proved users preferred the convenience of a device being able to autonomously triage for them.

How Does it Work?

An autonomous system is the key to this concept; combining a triaging smartwatch application, smart ambulance and an autonomous equipment allocation system. When the watch makes the autonomous decision that the user requires immediate medical attention, a call will be prompted. If the user responds, an alarm will be sent to the ambulance service with the system autonomously analysing the received data and equipping the ambulance with appropriate specialised equipment if needed. The user’s data such as the information on their injury and their medical history will be available for the EMTs to access on route within the ambulance.

The injured user will be able to track the ambulance live with an estimated time of arrival as well as its distance away. First aid tips tailored to the injury will be provided to advise users of what to do whilst waiting, allowing the user to regain some control in an otherwise helpless situation.

Design 1

Introduction

As the first high fidelity prototype, this version was the first to be created on InVision, built using feedback from numerous paper prototype iterations. A total of 7 users were tested, 3 tutors (Ashlynn, Jackson & Sophie), 2 regular users (Pansy & Eugene) and 2 medical professionals (Stephanie & Lesha). All users were tested individually to ensure that their opinions would not be influenced by any peers. The three DECO2200 tutors were tested during the time allocated for feedback during the studio on university grounds. Regular users were categorised as users who did not study design computing or relevant fields; thus having less context for what the application was to achieve. Eugene (IT solution architect) tested the product at his home through his personal laptop whilst Pansy (university student studying mathematics) tested the application on the university campus at the Carslaw building. The two medical professionals Stephanie (medical scribe) and Lesha (ED nurse) were unable to test in person due to their busy schedules and instead accessed the prototypes through the links generated on invision. These two participants were tested individually with each recorded on call as they explored the prototype using the think-aloud method to express their needs/dislikes.

Results

All participants with the exception of Jackson & Sophie had tested previous iterations of this prototype in the form of a paper prototype, requiring the aforementioned duo to be informed on the context of the application before user testing was commenced. Through testing all users were able to understand the purpose of the application and most importantly, understand how the flow worked; successfully navigating the interface without any major difficulties.

Recommendations

Majority of participants had many additional suggestions and recommendations to further improve the application. For the mobile application more information for the patient’s benefit such as allergies, private health insurance, blood type (positive or negative) and the ability to notify emergency contacts were added to the application. Heuristic issues were also addressed with visibility issues through colour choices (dot-loader for onboarding tutorial screens), help and documentation being added to numerous pages (myGov login, user information, history of cancelled calls, fingerprint biometrics, etc) with fonts differentiating information through methods such as italics and bolded text. User control and freedom as well as error prevention was something many users were concerned with, suggesting additional buttons of prompts to retrieve forgotten passwords and the option to edit profiles. There was also a prompt to add emergency contacts to the application, alerting specific contacts when an ambulance was called for the user.

The watch application had numerous suggestions from users also, with the most prominent request being the ability to check for general practitioners near the user through the watch interface. This would be especially effective for scenarios where the user encountered a minor injury that did not warrant the calling of an ambulance, this would still provide users with the ability to seek professional medical attention.Another recommendation was a functionality for the user to manually trigger the scan, in the scenario that they had turned minor injuries alert off or did not feel well. Certain icons were also suggested to be changed to better match between system and the real world, such as additional labels to prompt users.

Design 2

Introduction

Following the recommendations of users from the first iteration of the prototype, numerous features were added with the most notable being the inclusion of a list of GPs near the user’s location, an option for the user to scan themselves on the status page on the smartwatch application; as well as setting up an emergency contact on the mobile application. A total of four users were tested, with two participants having participated in the previous round and two new users added. Two regular users were tested with Eugene (IT solution architect, previous participant) testing the prototype under the same conditions as the first iteration. Wendy (university student studying psychology) tested the prototype using their personal computer on university grounds, being the first time they had interacted with the application. There were also two medical staff involved in testing with Stephanie (medical scribe) and Evan (EMT). Both participants were not able to test in person due to their busy schedules but was able to access the InVision prototype links through their personal computers and provided feedback through a call where their comments were transcribed. Eugene (architect) and Stephanie (medical scribe) were recruited as participants once more as the two had numerous suggestions in the previous iteration that had been implemented in this current design and it would be interesting to see their opinions on said changes.

Results

All participants were able to understand the flow of the applications and successfully navigate without any difficulties. Regular user Wendy (psychology student) had never interacted with this type of application previously and had to be provided context on how this application would function. Evan (EMT) had also never tested any previous prototype iterations but had some context of the application due to being interviewed for the research report. The comments were mostly positive as the users were able to interact with both prototypes, with majority of feedback being in form of further suggestions.

Recommendations

Further customisation was requested on the application such as the inclusion of a profile picture to the mobile profile section. Stephanie (medical scribe) highlighted the importance of this option as despite all the information provided it would be much easier for the EMTs to identify the user through a picture rather than data solely in the form of words or numbers. The importance of consistency and standards in the platform was commented upon through certain phrases needing to be rephrased, the layout of dates being universal, as well as the cost for cancelled ambulances being displayed. To create a better match between system and the real world and to prevent errors, it was highly suggested that the word ‘status’ on the watch homepage be reworded to ‘vitals’. Evan (EMT) recommended the avoidance of brand names in parts of the application (i.e ‘bandaid’) as well as to provide a disclaimer that first aid tips were not meant to replace a medical professional’s attention and that users should consult with doctors whenever possible. Both medical professionals also highly suggested making the application more accessible to people who may otherwise have a more difficult time due to colour blindness.

Final Design

Introduction

FAs the final iteration of ‘Smart Triaging’ it was of utmost importance that all user feedback was carefully considered, including reflection on recommendations in previous iterations that may have been overlooked. A profile picture option was integrated to the mobile prototype as well as the suggestions on phrasings of medical terms to be consistent with what the medical industry used. An additional option of a ‘colour-blind mode’ was added in the settings of the watch application. The three users that tested the design consisted of one recurring user Stephanie (medical scribe) and two new regular users Jason (accountant) and Andrew (English teacher). All three users were tested in a casual setting off-campus, using a personal computer to access the prototypes through InVision links. The observation was done in person to see their reactions and to provide any assistance that may be required.

Results

The most difficulty the new users had was understanding the concept of an autonomous system as they had not encountered any instances of this type of technology previously. However they were able to smoothly operate both prototype applications without having to ask for help when actually testing both. All three users enjoyed the fact that they were able to add a profile picture with Stephanie appreciating that her feedback was taken into serious consideration.

Recommendations

Due to the limits of InVision, despite the colour-blind mode being added on the watch interface, it is not able to be interacted with. In the future it would be ideal if this feature could be developed upon as well as other accessibility features. However as the main application is on a smartwatch, the option for larger text was regarded as unnecessary due to this being a typical existing optimisation option on smartwatch interfaces. There were also suggestions to further develop on the general practitioner functionalities such as adding the user’s regular GP to the list to easily contact them, in addition to showing the user’s nearest GPs. O2 sat was another stat suggestion to be displayed in the vital section as many fitness watches already have a pulse oximetry function, with low O2 sat levels potentially improving the triaging capabilities of the application.
