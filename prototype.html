PROCESS JOURNAL

Introduction

As the population continues to grow, the demand for ambulance services is predicted to increase in a similar fashion. To keep up with this ever-growing demand, the current ambulance service system must be revised and further assessed. As we slowly start to welcome an autonomous technology driven future, there is potential to improve ambulance services even further by streamlining the procedure and improve response and further benefiting society. This project focuses on exploring how autonomous technologies can be integrated in emergency ambulance services to improve the overall process and experience.

1. Background Research

Figure 1.1 - Sally using an academic database

To better understand the context of our study and identify all problem areas, a combination of literary, interview and survey research regarding the current state of ambulance services was conducted. Mapping out all stakeholders involved with ambulance services brought us three major user groups: medical staff involved with ambulance services, caretakers, and regular users who have experienced calling or using ambulance services before. To cover as much ground as possible, each member individually chose one user group for the first stage of research, where I covered caretakers.

Research: First Stage

6 semi-structured interviews were conducted with 5 aged care workers and 1 primary school teacher to gain qualitative data on the perspective of those who are legally responsible for the wellbeing of another person. Questions regarding their experience and personal opinions on any services they've received and what factors contribute to a successful service were asked, providing further insight into any latent or tacit needs. Analysis of the generally positive feedback collected resulted in the emergence of a central theme: the importance of communication and resources. Compiling this data with the rest of the team led to similar findings throughout all three user groups.

Research: Second Stage

To support the primary data collected, further secondary research was conducted regarding these two emergent themes. Past studies presented statistical data and key results from not only caretakers but a variety of perspectives, providing deeper insight into the two main areas of communication and resources. Within these areas, specific issues arose:

  • majority of emergency calls being deemed non-urgent or low acuity,
  • and the vital role communication plays in improving preparation, teamwork and service.

    • Research: Third Stage

    A survey was conducted to gain a deeper understanding into the work experience of paramedics. The quantitative data collected would help provide additional support to the secondary research as well as provide statistical data specific to the key problem areas. Results showed that 48% of the 38 respondents found their services often not actually being medically required, with 49% feeling frustrated because of this.

    Identifying Problem Areas

    By compiling all of the research data with the rest of the team, it was clear that the key issues found where common with everyone. The first being that, majority of calls are classified as non-urgent, which ultimately puts unnecessary strain on emergency service resources. Additionally communication was found to be a vital contributor to a positive experience. Whether it be communication between dispatchers and patients, or between paramedics and dispatchers, lack of communication often resulted in confusion and delays in service.

    Using these key themes we conducted a brainstorming session regarding characteristics that we believe important to consider in our solution, and how it would tie in with user needs. Our combined research contains a wide range of perspectives so mapping out our thoughts on the same page helped provide a visualisation of how various themes and characteristics link to one another.

    User Needs Brainstorming Session

    Ideation

    After figuring out the user needs, the ideation stage begun for potential solutions. While churning out as many ideas as possible, each member added their solutions to the page. No matter how improbable they may seem, these ideas would serve as a warm up exercise to allow us to get in the right mood and context, and uncover as many possible solutions as possible.

    Each member chose their two best solutions and wrote a brief summary describing their chosen concepts. Passing the page along, members then added features they thought would improve the idea, continually building and expanding upon the base of the original design. Pros and cons were also added and discussed, to give us an idea of what the benefits of choosing this solution would be, as well as the possible limitations we would face. After thorough discussion of these pros and cons, our 3 optimal solutions were chosen. The chosen ideas were: an ambulance triaging mobile application, a smart ambulance, and an autonomous equipment allocation system.

    However we felt that these ideas were still lacking as we despite basing our ideas around our initial mind map of user needs and issues, we found the incorporation of our central theme, autonomous technologies, was very minimal for all of the designs. Thus, another round of ideation brainstorming took place, again each choosing their best idea to create a new set of 3. These new features were: medical chips, AI Recognition street cameras and autonomous traffic lights. After thoroughly discussing the potential of these new ideas, we saw potential to integrate these features with our original 3 ideas.

    Final Solution

    By incorporating various features, we decided upon creating an autonomous ecosystem.

    Via interacting with an interface, patients' injuries are triaged, prompting them to call ambulances when appropriate. The patient’s location, injury data and medical history would be analysed by the system to make autonomous decisions. Based on these decisions, any special equipment needed would be autonomously allocated to an autonomous ambulance via a rail system. This provides paramedics with vital information on the patient, and ensures the paramedics are thoroughly prepared before arrival.

    On route, the system will analyse and generate the most efficient route to reach the patient, changing traffic lights and improving traffic flow in the ambulance’s favour. The system additionally identifies the nearest hospital that would be able to take in the patient, creating a route from the scene to the hospital.

    This system would incorporate various features from our brainstorm sessions including the autonomous traffic lights and autonomous equipment allocation.

    Interfaces

    It was decided that to benefit staff and help improve quality of service, we needed to target regular callers as they are the main reason that these services are needed in the first place, and are the main users that dictate the flow of the system. Three interfaces were designed to interact with the system:

  • Futuristic street lamps that used sensors and cameras to autonomously detect and triage certain injuries in public areas, with this solution taking much influence from AI recognition.
  • A smartwatch application that would use sensors to detect bodily injuries on the wearer, with this solution taking much influence from the idea of medical chips.
  • Mobile triaging application that would use AI recognition to scan and detect any injuries or illnesses.

    • Complications

    Upon further development and discussion, we discovered that our streetlamp design would not be able to cater for indoor cases. Though initially designed for outdoor use, a large number of calls do take place indoor, such as 9.7% of calls being made up of elderly fall patients (Cox, et al., 2018). For the reason that this idea could not cover indoor areas, it was decided that we could no longer continue with this design.

    Ideation Drawbacks and Reflections

    These rounds of ideation and brainstorming consumed more time than we had hoped, becoming definite setbacks for the team, towards both the schedule and our motivation. A considerable amount of time was spent coming up with a sound solution that considered not only the problem areas that we discovered, but also implemented autonomous technology. While the current state of autonomous technology is seeing slow but steady advancements, the idea has yet to become properly implemented in society.

    One thing we had to learn was to accept the futuristic nature of the context and to allow ourselves to think broader. Despite encouraging as many solutions as possible no matter how farfetched they may seem, when selecting probable features it was difficult to determine the line that separated the worlds of innovation, and outright science fiction. Due to our unfamiliarity with autonomous technology and its possibilities, we were unsure of what seemed plausible in the coming future. Additionally, novelty and originality was a personal goal for the team, even if there was no specific requirement for such. This led to hours of civil discourse within the group as we thoroughly discussed each and every idea's potential and limitations.

    In spite of all the time it took to finally come to a convincing agreement, all of these discussions really benefited us in the end, as it forced us to think deeper about each idea and contemplate various factors such as the necessity, the possibility, and the feasibility of these ideas. Our analysis made sure that we put extra thought into every aspect, so that we could only pick our best options.

    User Testing

    For both solutions various screens were designed and tested with various users. Pre-testing surveys were conducted to identify our participants and their familiarity with the context presented, such as their experience with smartwatches and health related apps. Post-testing surveys were also held to collect statistical ratings of our designs.

    Think aloud testing was conducted to capture the organic thought process each participant had when interacting with the designs. Each participant was asked to complete a set of tasks where their time, errors, comments and observations were recorded as feedback. Three stages of testing were held with 20 unique users overall, with some participating in numerous rounds. This allowed us to collect feedback from new perspectives as well evaluations on any adjustments we made. It was also ensured that a participant from the medical industry was involved with testing for each round. These comparisons from a wide range of participants allowed for deeper insights into how users interacted with our interfaces.

    Users tested with paper prototypes made to emulate our designed interfaces. While we attempted to visually represent the screens as accurately as was possible, there was still some difficulty in effectively representing digital interactions on paper for aspects such as camera scanning functions and animation elements. However despite this we believe were still able to collect thorough and rich data in spite of this issue.

    User Testing: First Stage

    The first round helped us identify many flaws with our mobile application. Issues with screen flow were presented when users were asked to triage for other people. Many found it difficult to comprehend the need for logging out, and failed to recognise logged in statuses. Only after explanations were given did they understand the need for this.

    Due to the limited screen size of the smartwatch the main task we faced was designing an interface that was easy to navigate and access. While these issues were considered, navigation problems still presented themselves, with majority of users not being experienced with smartwatches and so found difficulty navigating due to the lack of instructions provided. However as smartwatches are catered for the wearer where triaging for different people was not an option, participants were quicker in understanding and using the smartwatch application, and were actually more in favour of the smartwatch.

    User Testing: Second Stage

    For the second round the solutions were iterated upon with screen flow being adjusted and icons being redesigned, in which we received more balanced results this time.

    The mobile application saw a redesign in the triage process, which found much greater success than the first iteration. As we removed the need to log out and created a home page, users found it easier to understand switch between triaging for themselves and others. With the addition of a few new screens and change in screen flow, users found themselves lacking sufficient explanations of certain features. Users felt that more information was required, which tied into the need for further help and documentation.

    Other than a few navigation issues, we still found that users found task completion easier on smartwatches, simply because the idea of wearables being used for medical purposes was believable as is common in society today. Thus users found themselves understanding the convenience of the smartwatch concept more than the mobile application. And for that reason we ultimately decided for our final solution to be the smartwatch application.

    However smartwatch applications must have a mobile counterpart and do not typically work as a standalone smartwatch application. So we decided to retain the login and account creation process of the mobile application. Our final solution would now consist of using the mobile application to create an account, while using the smartwatch for triaging and calling functions.

    User Testing: Third Stage

    The screen designs went through a final round of iteration to improve clarity and create a high fidelity wireframe design that implemented specific aesthetic choices such as fonts and colour. This was done by using universal icons, as it was pointed out that some instructions such as 'OK' and 'Next' seemed vague in interpretation of what screen or direction it would take them to. Additionally, initial onboarding tutorial screens explaining the app functions was added to the mobile application.

    Users were successful with navigating their way with both the mobile and smartwatch counterparts. Participants working in the medical industry advised for wording and visual hierarchy to be revised, as the choice of wording presented seemed to be misleading or open to misinterpretation, as well as believing that certain text elements are of higher priority than the icons.

    Testing: Reflections

    While we carefully chose the right questions to collect relevant data, it is possible that a few of the questions were a bit difficult to assess. Likert scale questions such as "how effective do you think this would be in a non-lethal emergency?" or "how easy was it to navigate?" were honestly at times difficult to gauge for a few users. Though these responses were necessary for our evaluations, it is possible that users' could not accurately answer due to themes such as "effectiveness" being hard to represent through a 5 point Likert scale. Additionally the fact that people typically choose the middle option when they wish to avoid answering a question is a problem that may are may not have affected the accuracy of the results.

    Next Steps

    Based off of the feedback received from our final testing stage, appropriate modifications will be made to our high fidelity wireframes. We aim to develop the high-fidelity mock-up using InVision to emulate the proper digital interactive experience as accurately as possible.
